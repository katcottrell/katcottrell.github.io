What are Racket, Lisp, and Scheme?

“The list, Lisp's native data structure, is largely responsible for such growth of utility. The simple structure and natural applicability of lists are reflected in functions that are amazingly nonidiosyncratic.”

—Alan J. Perlis, Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs

Lisp stands for “list processing.” Popularly known for their extensive use of parentheses, Lisp languages were originally used largely for research in artificial intelligence.

  • 1958 - Lisp was created by John McCarthy at MIT. Lisp is now one of the oldest families of programming languages still in common use.
  • 1970’s - Scheme, a dialect of Lisp, was created by Guy L. Steele and Gerald Jay Sussman at the MIT AI Lab.
  • 1995 - PLT Inc created what later came to be known as Racket. Racket is based on Scheme and was created for teaching purposes. PLT Inc also created DrScheme, an environment for Racket.

Lisp is used mostly for applications that can make good use of its power for processing lists and similar data structures. Racket is used mostly in pedagogical settings, as the structure of the language cleanly parallels core ideas in computer science, such as data structures, eval/apply, recursion, conditionals, typing, and memory.

How do I know Racket?
